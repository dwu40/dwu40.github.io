Women Astronauts

Since the early beginnings of human spaceflight in the second half of twentieth century, women have been participants of the space exploration experience. On June 16, 1963, two years after the first human had reached outer space, the Soviet Union launched the Vostok 6 spacecraft, and along with it was 26-year-old Valentina Tereshkova, who became the first woman in space. As the years progressed, so have human achievements in outer space. The space race between the Soviet Union and the United States may have peaked as the first human landed on the moon on July 20, 1969, but the rarity of women in space programs had remained the same. It was not until 1982, nearly twenty years after the first woman in space, that Svetlana Savitskaya, aboard the Soyuz T-5 of the Soviet Union, had become the second woman in space. In 1984, she became the first woman to make a second spaceflight, and she was also the first woman to participate in a spacewalk, also known as an extravehicular activity (EVA). Since then, the number of women participants in space exploration programs around the world have grown.

Shortly after Savitskaya’s first expedition, Sally Ride became the third woman in space, and the first American woman in space on June 18, 1983. Helen Sharman became the first woman astronaut from the United Kingdom on May 18, 1991. Roberta Bondar became the first woman astronaut from Canada on January 22, 1992. Chiaki Mukai became the first woman astronaut from Japan on July 8, 1994. Claudie Haignere became the first woman astronaut from France on August 17, 1996. Anousheh Ansari became the first woman astronaut from Iran on September 18, 2006. Yi So-yeon became the first astronaut from South Korea on April 8, 2008. Liu Yang became the first woman astronaut from China on June 16, 2012. Samantha Cristoforetti became the first woman astronaut from Italy on November 23, 2014.

Still, there have been fewer than a hundred women astronauts to date. As of March 2020, there were sixty-five women astronauts who have flown in space. While this may account for a little more than 10% of all astronauts who have been to outer space, women have nonetheless made their mark in the history of human spaceflight. In 1999, on flight STS-93, Eileen Collins became the first woman space shuttle commander, the highest rank under NASA’s Space Shuttle program. In 2001, Susan Helms became the first woman crew member aboard the International Space Station (ISS). In 2008, Peggy Whitson became the first woman to command the ISS.
